# VIA Cervical Cancer Screening — Clinical Reference

**IMPORTANT: This is reference material only. No personalised analysis is available. Escalate to your supervisor immediately.**

---

## What is VIA?

Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid (VIA) is a low-cost cervical cancer screening method. Dilute acetic acid (3–5%) is applied to the cervix. Pre-cancerous lesions turn white (acetowhite) within 1 minute.

---

## How to Interpret What You See

### VIA Negative (Normal)
- **No acetowhite areas** visible, OR
- Indistinct, faint acetowhite areas far from the os that disappear quickly
- **Action:** Reassure patient. Routine rescreening in 3 years.

### VIA Positive (Abnormal — Refer)
- **Well-defined acetowhite area** close to or touching the squamocolumnar junction (SCJ) or os
- Dense, opaque, thick white areas
- Acetowhite area that persists for more than 2 minutes
- **Action:** Refer immediately for colposcopy. If eligible and trained, consider same-visit cryotherapy.

### Suspicious for Cancer — Refer Urgently
- Ulcerative or cauliflower-like lesion
- Contact bleeding from cervix
- Irregular, raised mass
- **Action:** Urgent referral to higher-level facility. Do NOT delay.

---

## When in Doubt

- **Always REFER if uncertain.** A false positive referral is far less harmful than a missed cancer.
- If image quality is poor (blurry, underexposed, overexposed), retake the image or perform a direct clinical examination.
- If acetic acid application time was insufficient (< 60 seconds), repeat the procedure.

---

## Post-Procedure

- Record all findings in the encounter log.
- Inform the patient of the result in plain language.
- Ensure the patient understands the next steps.
- For REFER cases: confirm the referral appointment before the patient leaves.

---

## Emergency Signs — Call for Help Immediately

- Heavy bleeding that does not stop
- Patient loses consciousness
- Signs of severe allergic reaction to acetic acid
